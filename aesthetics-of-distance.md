The Aesthetics of Distance — Millet, Van Gogh, and Faces

by Jiseung Shin

Inside the digital frame hanging on the wall of the library cafeteria, masterpieces of Impressionism repeat endlessly in meaningless mechanical cycles throughout the day. Degas, Monet, Van Gogh. Yet to most people, they seem no more than decorative wallpaper in an indifferent public dining room. As I eat, I find myself staring at those paintings and falling into thought.

Recently, I bought a reproduction of Monet’s Woman with a Parasol on Temu for five dollars. In the painting, Camille and their son Jean stand atop a hill, looking down through sunlight and wind. When I hung the painting above the entrance of my home, the distance between the work and myself suddenly became closer. Yet to my children, it probably felt no different from the distance I once felt as an elementary school student staring at Millet’s The Angelus hanging above the mirror in a neighborhood barbershop.

Perhaps I bought Monet’s painting because it felt cinematic. There is even a Japanese animated film poster strangely similar to it.

During my elementary school years, The Angelus always hung above the large mirror in my regular barbershop. Two peasants bowing their heads beneath the evening sky in prayer. For six years, I looked at that image every month while getting my hair cut. Did I know who painted it? Probably not.

Yet over time the painting became strangely close to me. By the end of those years, whenever I entered the shop, I would instinctively glance around searching for it.

Through middle school and university, I never formed a particularly intimate relationship with painting. At most, images from Buddhist iconography — the Four Heavenly Kings or the Ox-Herding Pictures — briefly passed through my field of vision at my Buddhist middle school. During university, it was Käthe Kollwitz’s engraved images of resistance, stripped even of laughter, that decorated my youth.

Later, after entering cinema, I encountered An Gyeon’s Dream Journey to the Peach Blossom Land. I became possessed by the desire to recreate that fantastical landscape through film. I bought a 35mm Russian camera, gathered leftover film stock discarded from commercial productions, and brought enormous lights and crew members into a peach orchard.

But after two nights and three days, I failed to complete the film.

Every scene took place at night. Even after staying awake for two full nights, I could not shoot quickly enough. I remember crying uncontrollably, overwhelmed by the limits of my own ability. Perhaps that was the moment I unconsciously decided never again to pursue filmmaking enslaved by money. Instead, I may have begun sharpening, through long nights, the ideological blade of what I would later call “Stone Tower Cinema” — a cinema wandering freely, unbound by capital.

At some point, Millet’s The Angelus ceased to be a familiar sacred image and returned instead as an unsettling question. I had once identified with Millet simply as a poor artist. But later I learned that, by middle age, he had become a successful bourgeois intellectual painter, raising nine children while moving across borders as a refugee during periods of unrest.

I felt something close to betrayal.

He was not a farmer laboring beside peasants in the fields. He was an observer reconstructing them within his studio as sacred archetypes.

Nameless Prayer and Named Faces

The couple in The Angelus are clearly husband and wife, yet we never learn their names. Their posture of prayer appears solemn, but that solemnity floats above the specific realities of suffering, frozen eternally beneath a beautiful sunset.

Salvador Dalí once argued that the basket in the painting was originally a child’s coffin, that the scene depicted parents mourning their dead child in prayer. X-ray analysis later revealed underdrawings resembling a coffin beneath the basket.

If that interpretation is true, then Millet transformed even the most devastating reality of peasant life into a universalized image of spiritual dignity.

Millet painted peasants, but he never truly approached them. He depicted the weight of the earth without summoning the singular voices of those who carried it. And so the figures remain permanently trapped within the category of “peasants.” They exist within the observer’s gaze, but not within the heat of relationship.

Of course, every artist remains bound by the limitations of their historical moment — by class structures, cultural codes, religious frameworks, and the distance their era allowed them to endure. Perhaps it is pointless to hold too much disappointment toward them.

Van Gogh, by contrast, painted people with names.

Living among the poor as their neighbor, he recorded rough knuckles and hollowed eyes alongside individual identities. Among them, the postman Joseph Roulin became more than a model; he became a presence sustaining Van Gogh’s soul itself.

Roulin stepped beyond the canvas and entered Van Gogh’s actual life, drinking with him, enduring his anxieties and breakdowns beside him.

Van Gogh could not reduce people into symbols or archetypes. He stood too close to them. Because of that closeness, his paintings carry a singular pulse — the warmth and instability of human relationship itself.

If Millet’s peasants resemble sacred icons suspended outside time, Van Gogh’s figures still feel capable of speaking to us as living neighbors.

Stone Tower Cinema: Building an Aesthetics of Relationship

The distance between Millet and Van Gogh is not merely a difference in style. It is a difference in artistic ethics.

Millet observed humanity from afar. Van Gogh stepped dangerously close. One preserved dignity through distance but remained silent. The other chose relationship and nearly burned himself away within it.

“Stone Tower Cinema,” the form of cinema I pursue, begins precisely at this tension.

It neither imprisons people as anonymous archetypes like Millet, nor destroys itself entirely within the fever of intimacy like Van Gogh. Instead, it attempts to preserve the singular names and faces of individuals.

Within this cinema, people are not objects of observation. They become collaborators — companions who think through local questions and create together.

In Monet’s Woman with a Parasol, Camille and Jean stand within radiant sunlight. Monet loved that fleeting light, yet when Camille later lay dying from illness, he obsessively painted her approaching death as well.

I have always been deeply moved by Monet’s willingness to embrace both beauty and decay within the same life.

And yet, for hanging inside a home, Woman with a Parasol still feels easier to live beside than the paintings of Camille dying.

Like stacking stones one by one to build a tower, we build cinema through relationships with one another. The artist must resist consuming people unilaterally. Instead, through exchange and mutual recognition, stories rooted in the earth slowly emerge.

I have long asked myself: where should my cinema stand?

Should it preserve people through formal distance like Millet? Or enter the dangerous risks of intimacy like Van Gogh?

In Stone Tower Cinema, residents are not merely filmed subjects. The process begins by discussing stories together, sitting face to face. They participate as crew members during filming. Through frequent screenings of unfinished footage, they observe how their lives are being transformed into cinematic and even metaphysical forms. Finally, through village festivals, they witness their stories becoming complete as films.

This is not observation. It is collaboration.

Not representation, but collective creation.

Together with villagers — on their land, within their time, through their rhythms — we build a cinematic tower stone by stone.

I dream of transforming this into the seed of a global network of stone towers responding to the globalization of platforms like Netflix.

The depth of art is always determined within the tension of distance.

Too far away, and people become preserved decorations. Too close, and the artist burns into ash.

What matters is enduring that unstable tension — continuing to walk beside singular faces without ceasing, continuing to tremble within relationship itself.
